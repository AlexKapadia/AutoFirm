## Key claims (faithful, with exact terms)

### The two pillars of TPS
1. **Just-in-Time (JIT):** producing/conveying "only what is needed, when it is needed, and in the
   amount needed."
2. **Jidoka (自働化) — "autonomation" / automation with a human touch:** a machine/worker stops
   automatically when an abnormality/defect occurs, so defects are not passed downstream. Ohno
   distinguishes 自働化 (jidoka, "human" radical 人) from ordinary 自動化 (automation).

### The seven wastes (muda) — Ohno's classification
1. **Overproduction** (Ohno: the *worst* waste, it hides the others)
2. **Waiting** (time on hand)
3. **Transportation / conveyance**
4. **Over-processing / processing itself**
5. **Inventory** (excess stock)
6. **Motion** (unnecessary movement of people)
7. **Defects** (making defective products → rework)

> Mnemonic **TIMWOOD** (Transport, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Over-processing, Overproduction,
> Defects) is a later Western teaching device, NOT Ohno's original ordering.

### The three M's
- **Muda (無駄)** — waste (the seven). **Mura (斑)** — unevenness in volume/mix.
- **Muri (無理)** — overburden of people/equipment. Reducing mura/muri precedes durably removing muda.

### Supporting mechanisms (as described by Ohno)
- **Kanban (看板):** signaling system that operationalizes pull; downstream withdraws exactly what it
  needs. Kanban is the means; JIT is the goal. Ohno warns kanban without underlying improvement is dangerous.
- **Pull (vs push):** information flows upstream from final assembly / customer demand.
- **Heijunka (平準化):** production levelling — smoothing volume/mix to reduce mura.
- **5 Whys:** ask "why" five times to reach the true root cause.
- **Genchi genbutsu / go-and-see (現地現物):** verify facts at the actual place.
