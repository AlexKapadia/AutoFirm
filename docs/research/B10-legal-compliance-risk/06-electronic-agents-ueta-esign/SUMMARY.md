# SUMMARY — Electronic agents & contract attribution (UETA / E-SIGN) in the age of agentic AI

  **§2(6)** (definition of "electronic agent"), **§14** (automated transactions), and §14 Comment 1.
  (Model act adopted by ~49 US states.)
- **Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN), 2000.** Pub. L. 106-229,
  15 U.S.C. §7001 et seq.

  (professional law-firm legal analysis applying UETA/E-SIGN to AI agents — the integrating source.)

## Key claims (with locators, exact quotes)
1. **"Electronic agent" defined** (UETA §2(6), verbatim): *"a computer program or an electronic or
   other automated means used independently to initiate an action or respond to electronic records in
   whole or in part, without review or action by an individual."* → **Agentic AI qualifies.**
2. **Automated contracts are valid** (UETA §14): a contract may be formed by the interaction of
   electronic agents **"even if no individual was aware of or reviewed the electronic agents' actions"**
   (§14(1)).
3. **Attribution / who is bound** (UETA §14 Comment 1, per Proskauer): contractual intent derives from
   **"the programming and use of the machine"**; the UETA prefatory note states automated tools
   **"programmed and used by people will bind the user of the machine."** → **The deployer/user, not the
   AI, is the contracting party bound.**
4. **E-SIGN attribution condition** (15 U.S.C. §7001; per Proskauer, verbatim): an electronic agent may
   form a contract **"so long as the action of any such electronic agent is legally attributable to the
   person to be bound."**
5. **Open legal gap (Proskauer):** the "programming and use" standard **presumes programmer intent
   aligns with user intent** — problematic for highly autonomous agents that act in ways the user did
   not specifically authorize. Apparent-authority doctrine (agency law) is flagged as the unresolved
   frontier (deferred to the firm's Part III).
