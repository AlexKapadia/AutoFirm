# SUMMARY — The FAST Standard (FAST Standard Organisation)

## Full citation
- **Title:** *The FAST Standard* (current published version; FAST = Flexible, Appropriate, Structured, Transparent)
- **Author/Org:** The FAST Standard Organisation (FSO) — a not-for-profit; standard published openly and periodically revised.
- **Year:** Living standard (originated ~2008 by the FAST Alliance; current revisions ongoing).
- **Venue:** fast-standard.org (open publication; FAST Level 1 Certificate is the conformance credential).
- **URL/DOI:** https://fast-standard.org/

## Key claims (faithful)
1. **FAST = Flexible, Appropriate, Structured, Transparent** — four principles for spreadsheet model design:
   - **Flexible:** key drivers/assumptions can be changed quickly while model integrity remains intact.
   - **Appropriate:** the model's accuracy/complexity is matched to its purpose (not over-engineered).
   - **Structured:** systematic, consistent layout, formula use, and documentation → navigable, maintainable, error-checkable.
   - **Transparent:** straightforward formulas, clear labelling, comprehensive documentation explaining the rationale of each element.
2. **Rigorous consistency in layout/organisation** is essential to preserve logical integrity over time, "particularly as a model's author may change" — a consistent approach to workbooks/worksheets/formulas saves time building, learning, and maintaining.
3. Adopting the standard "increases your productivity, reduces errors within your models, and makes them easier to understand and review."
4. Operational rules (the canonical FAST style): inputs flow left→right and top→down; **one row = one consistent calculation**; avoid hard-coded numbers inside formulas; keep formulas short and readable (prefer many simple steps over one mega-formula); consistent colour-coding (inputs vs calculations); time series flow consistently across columns.

## Reproducibility note
The four FAST principles and the consistency/transparency rules are openly published at fast-standard.org and corroborated by Grossman & Ozluk (2010) and the Full Stack Modeller / Gridlines practitioner literature. Cross-checked 2026-06-15.
