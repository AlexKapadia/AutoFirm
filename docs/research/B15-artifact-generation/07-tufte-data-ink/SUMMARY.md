# SUMMARY — Tufte: Data-Ink Ratio and Chartjunk

## Full citation
- **Title:** *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information*
- **Author:** Edward R. Tufte
- **Year:** 1983 (2nd ed. 2001), Graphics Press.
- **Venue:** Book (the primary, canonical reference in information design).
- **URL/DOI:** Primary = the book. Pointers: InfoVis-Wiki https://infovis-wiki.net/wiki/Data-Ink_Ratio ; Chartjunk https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartjunk

## Key claims (faithful, exact wording)
1. **Data-ink (definition, Tufte 1983):** "the non-erasable core of a graphic, the non-redundant ink arranged in response to variation in the numbers represented."
2. **Data-ink ratio (definition):** "the ratio of the data-ink over the total ink used in a graphic" — equivalently, the proportion of a graphic's ink devoted to data. Tufte expresses it as **data-ink ratio = data-ink / total ink used to print the graphic**, which also equals **1.0 minus the proportion of a graphic that can be erased without loss of data-information**.
3. **Core principle:** "Above all else show the data." Maximize the data-ink ratio (within reason) — i.e. push it toward 1.0 without erasing anything necessary for effective communication.
4. **Chartjunk (term coined by Tufte, 1983):** "ink that does not tell the viewer anything new" — non-data-ink or redundant data-ink; decorative elements that do not aid (and may distract from) comprehension. Examples: heavy gridlines, 3D effects, moiré patterns, redundant labels, decorative imagery.
5. Corollary principles (Tufte): erase non-data-ink and redundant data-ink; avoid chartjunk; maximize data density.
