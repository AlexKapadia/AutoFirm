## Key claims (the eight principles — exact names + faithful gloss)
1. **Principle of Relevance:** present the right amount of information — "as much as your audience
   needs, but not more" (under- and over-loading both impair).
2. **Principle of Appropriate Knowledge:** pitch to the audience's existing knowledge; avoid jargon;
   use the audience's own words.
3. **Principle of Salience:** "attention is drawn to large perceptible differences" — the most
   important element should have the greatest contrast.
4. **Principle of Discriminability:** two properties must differ enough to be told apart (sufficient
   contrast in size/color/position).
5. **Principle of Perceptual Organization:** people group elements (Gestalt) — exploit proximity/
   similarity to "chunk" related content.
6. **Principle of Compatibility:** a message is easiest to grasp when its *form* matches its
   *meaning* (e.g. "more" shown as larger/higher).
7. **Principle of Informative Changes:** every change in a property should carry information (don't
   change color/size/style decoratively).
8. **Principle of Capacity Limitations:** working memory is limited — minimize what must be held in
   mind at once (limit items per slide).

## Reproducibility note
The eight principle names and glosses are reproduced from the published list and OUP description.
These map directly onto, and provide a cognitive justification for, existing B15.2 rules: Salience+
Discriminability ↔ IBCS/Tufte contrast and de-cluttering; Capacity Limitations ↔ Minto MECE / one
message per slide; Informative Changes ↔ Tufte data-ink (no decorative change) and IBCS UNIFY
(changes are semantic); Compatibility ↔ Zelazny message-type→chart-type matching. A reviewer
re-derives the corroboration by aligning each principle to its practitioner counterpart (above).
