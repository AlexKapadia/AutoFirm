## GRADE tier

**High** — peer-reviewed in Review of Economic Studies; the definitive empirical study of real VC
term sheets (213 financings, 119 companies, 14 VC firms). Primary, not survey-of-surveys. No
down-rate.

## Key claims

- **VC financings separately allocate** five distinct bundles of rights: **cash-flow rights, board
  rights, voting rights, liquidation rights, and other control rights** — and these are allocated
  *independently* and *state-contingently*.
- **Convertible preferred stock is the most frequently used security** (often convertible
  participating preferred); VCs achieve a similar allocation using combinations of multiple common
  classes and straight preferred when not using convertibles.
- **Cash-flow rights are tied to performance / milestones** (consistent with principal-agent theory):
  the entrepreneur's equity stake is more performance-sensitive when incentive and asymmetric-
  information problems are more severe.
- **Control shifts state-contingently:** if the firm performs poorly, the VC gets full control; as
  performance improves, the entrepreneur retains/regains control and cash-flow rights. Contracts are
  **inherently incomplete**, so allocating *control* rights (not just cash-flow rights) is central.

## Why it matters here

It defines the **real mechanics of an equity raise** AutoFirm must model: a raise is not just "sell
N% for $X" — it is a structured allocation of preferred-stock economics (liquidation preference,
participation, anti-dilution) and **control rights** (board seats, protective provisions, vesting).
The fundraising playbook must surface and explain these, not hide them.

## Reproducibility note

The five rights categories, the dominance of convertible preferred, and the state-contingent control
allocation are the paper's headline empirical findings, restated in the abstract and results tables.
