## GRADE tier

**High** — two peer-reviewed papers in the top finance journals (JF, JFE) by Stewart Myers (and
Majluf); the canonical statement of the pecking order. No down-rate.

## Key claims

- **Asymmetric information** between better-informed managers and less-informed outside investors
  drives a strict **financing hierarchy**:
  > 1. internal funds (retained earnings)  ->  2. debt  ->  3. (last resort) external equity
- **Mechanism (Myers-Majluf):** because managers issue equity when they believe it is overpriced,
  rational investors **discount** the price of any new equity issue. This adverse-selection discount
  makes external equity the *most expensive* source, so firms exhaust cheaper, less
  information-sensitive sources (internal cash, then debt) first. Equity issuance can even cause
  **underinvestment** — managers forgo positive-NPV projects rather than issue underpriced shares.
- **Contrast with trade-off theory:** the pecking order predicts **no target leverage ratio**;
  observed leverage is the cumulative result of past financing-deficit decisions. Profitable firms
  borrow less (they have more internal funds) — the opposite of naive trade-off tax logic, and a key
  empirical discriminator (see source 04).

## Reproducibility note

In the Myers-Majluf model, a firm with private information about asset value and a new project
chooses whether to issue equity; the equilibrium shows equity issues convey negative information, so
firms prefer internal funds then debt. The hierarchy and the "profitable firms borrow less"
prediction are the testable corollaries examined in source 04.
