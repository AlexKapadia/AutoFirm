## Key claims (exact findings)

- Six factors are **reliably important for market leverage** (sign in parentheses):
  median **industry leverage (+)**, **market-to-book** assets ratio **(-)**, **tangibility (+)**,
  **profits (-)**, **log of assets / firm size (+)**, and **expected inflation (+)**.
- **Dividend-paying firms tend to have lower leverage.**
- For **book leverage**, effects are similar but firm size, market-to-book, and inflation are **not
  reliable**.
- **Theory verdict:** "the empirical evidence seems reasonably consistent with some versions of the
  **trade-off theory** of capital structure." The negative profit-leverage sign is often read as
  pecking-order support, but the broad pattern (esp. industry-leverage targeting and tangibility)
  leans trade-off.

## Why it matters here

This is the **adjudicator** between sources 02 (trade-off) and 03 (pecking order): neither wins
universally, but for established firms with assets and profits, trade-off-style **target leverage**
behavior dominates the data, while the strong negative profit-leverage relation keeps the
pecking-order mechanism alive — supporting a **hybrid** stance (CLAUDE.md §3.5).

## Reproducibility note

Findings are from the paper's reliability analysis across 1950-2003 Compustat firms; a reviewer can
confirm the six core market-leverage factors and signs in the results tables.
