## Core claims / definitions (as described)
- **Core idea:** customers do not "buy products," they **"hire"** a product/service to make
  **progress** in a particular circumstance — to get a **"job" done**. "When we buy a product, we
  essentially hire it to do a job. If it does the job well, we hire it again; if it does a crummy
  job, we fire it and look for something else."
- **A "job to be done":** the **progress a person is trying to make in a given circumstance** —
  defined by the circumstance, not by customer demographics.
- **Three dimensions of a job:** **functional** (the practical task), **social** (how it makes the
  person appear to others), and **emotional** (how it makes them feel).
- **The milkshake example:** traditional segmentation profiled buyers (e.g. men 30-40 commuting);
  the JTBD lens asked *what job the milkshake was hired for* — a long, boring morning commute needed
  something filling, one-handed, slow to consume. Demographic correlation missed the causal job.
- **Method consequence:** stop correlating attributes; identify the **causal job** and design the
  product/marketing/experience around getting that job done.

## Relationship to other sources
This is the **"Jobs as Progress"** (Christensen) school; the **"Jobs as Activity"** (Ulwick / ODI)
school is source 06; the explicit two-schools debate is source 09. JTBD answers *which hypotheses
to test* (the job); Lean Startup (sources 03/04) answers *how to test them*.
