- **Moderate** — based on Eisenmann's multi-year research: "interviews and surveys with hundreds of
  founders and investors and scores of accounts of entrepreneurial setbacks." HBR venue (professional,
  not peer-reviewed empirical), but the pattern taxonomy is the field-leading scholar's synthesized

## Core claims (as described)
- **Thesis:** failure is NOT simply "bad team or bad idea." Eisenmann identifies **six recurring
  failure patterns** that account for the vast majority of startup failures:
  1. **Bad Bedfellows** — non-founder parties (employees, partners, investors) drive the failure.
  2. **False Starts** — founders **skip customer-needs research** and rush to build/test an MVP
     before understanding the problem — a **misapplication of the lean-startup process**.
  3. **False Promises** — early-adopter success misleads founders into premature expansion (the
     early market does not represent the mainstream).
  4. **Speed Traps** — "get big fast" / hypergrowth destroys promising ventures.
  5. **Help Wanted** — rapid scaling runs short of capital and/or talent at the wrong moment.
  6. **Cascading Miracles** — over-ambitious visions require too many independent things to all go
     right.
- **The "false start" critique (load-bearing for L1.B3.1):** many founders "overlook a crucial step
  in the lean start-up process: researching customer needs before testing products." They jump
  straight to building an MVP, skipping **customer discovery** — so they efficiently build and test
  the *wrong* thing. The fix is a discovery-first sequence (problem research BEFORE solution MVPs),
  i.e. doing Blank's Customer Discovery (source 04) before Build-Measure-Learn (source 03).

## Relationship to other sources
Directly critiques naive Lean Startup (sources 03/04): the method WORKS only if discovery precedes
build. Complements the RCT evidence (sources 01/02): rigorous hypothesis-testing helps, but only if
applied to the *right* (discovery-grounded) hypotheses.

## Reproducibility note
Qualitative synthesis from a large founder/investor evidence base; the six-pattern taxonomy and the
false-start critique are reproduced from Eisenmann's own framing. Conclusions are directional
(failure patterns), not causal effect sizes.
