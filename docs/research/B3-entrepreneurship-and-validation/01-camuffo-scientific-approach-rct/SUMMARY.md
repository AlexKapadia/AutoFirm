# SUMMARY — A Scientific Approach to Entrepreneurial Decision Making (RCT)

## Full citation
- **Title:** A Scientific Approach to Entrepreneurial Decision Making: Evidence from a Randomized Control Trial
- **Authors:** Arnaldo Camuffo; Alessandro Cordova; Alfonso Gambardella; Chiara Spina
- **Year:** 2020 (published online ahead of print 2019)
- **Venue:** *Management Science*, Vol. 66, No. 2, pp. 564-586 (INFORMS)
- **DOI:** 10.1287/mnsc.2018.3249 - URL: https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2018.3249
- **Open-access copy verified:** https://gwern.net/doc/economics/2019-camuffo.pdf
- **SSRN preprint:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3295625

## Ontology questions informed
- **L1.B3.1** - opportunity-discovery & validation theory: the strongest CAUSAL (RCT) evidence on
  whether a hypothesis-testing / "scientific" approach to entrepreneurship changes outcomes.

## GRADE tier
- **High** - randomized controlled trial (gold standard for causal inference), peer-reviewed at a
  top-tier management journal (*Management Science*). Up-rated: clean randomization, registered
  design, independently replicated (source 02). Down-rate caution: single national context (Italy),
  modest n, performance effect noisier than the behavioral effects.

## Study design (exact)
- **Sample:** 116 Italian early-stage startups in a pre-acceleration program, randomly assigned to
  a **treatment** group (taught to frame the business idea as a set of falsifiable hypotheses and
  test them rigorously - a "scientific approach") vs. a **control** group (taught conventional but
  equally intensive idea-development practices).
- **Both arms** received the same dosage: **10 sessions** of general training on getting market
  feedback and gauging idea feasibility; only the *framing* (scientific hypothesis-testing vs. not)
  differed. This isolates the causal effect of the scientific framing.
- **Tracking:** ~1 year, **16 data points** per firm (longitudinal panel).

## Key findings (exact, load-bearing)
1. **Treated entrepreneurs were more likely to PIVOT** their idea (change strategy in response to
   disconfirming evidence) than controls.
2. **Treated entrepreneurs were more likely to TERMINATE** a non-viable project early (stop sooner
   when data said the idea would not work), rather than persisting.
3. **The apparent paradox** (more pivots AND more terminations) resolves because rigorous hypothesis
   testing **accelerates learning about viability** - treated founders update faster, so they both
   abandon dead ends sooner and re-aim toward better ideas.
4. **Performance/revenue effect:** present but **noisier / higher variance** than the behavioral
   effects; the paper emphasizes *decision quality* as the primary robust outcome. In the small
   revenue sub-sample, 17 firms earned revenue (9 treatment, 8 control), so revenue conclusions are
   drawn cautiously; the larger replication (source 02) supplies the precise, significant estimate.

## Mechanism (as stated)
Framing a venture as **falsifiable hypotheses + disciplined tests** reduces over-optimistic
persistence (a known entrepreneurial cognitive bias) and produces **better-calibrated go/no-go
decisions**. The treatment does not make founders "succeed more" by magic - it makes them **decide
better**, which shows up as faster, more accurate pivots and terminations.

## Reproducibility note
RCT with pre-registered arms; effect directions (more pivots, more terminations) independently
reproduced at much larger scale (source 02, 759 firms / 4 RCTs). A reviewer can re-derive the
conclusion from the published treatment-effect tables in *Management Science* 66(2):564-586.
