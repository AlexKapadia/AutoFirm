## Ontology questions informed
- **L1.B3.1** — the meta-level evidence picture: what is actually known (and NOT known) about Lean
  Startup's effectiveness — the critique/limitations balance required by DEPTH-RUBRIC SS4.

## GRADE tier
- **Moderate** — systematic literature review (PRISMA-style synthesis) but a student/working-paper
  venue, not a top peer-reviewed journal; corroborated by independently published peer-reviewed SLRs
  (Frederiksen & Brem 2017; Ghezzi 2019). The *finding that empirical evidence is thin* is itself
  robustly corroborated across multiple SLRs.

## Core claims / findings
- **Lean Startup reduces to two central constructs:** **the MVP** and **pivoting** (Contigiani &
  Levinthal 2019), with **validated learning through purposeful experimentation** as the core
  process.
- **The evidence base is THIN and skewed:** "there is very little systematic empirical investigation
  on LS applicability and effectiveness in the real world"; most studies focus on **isolated settings**
  (single companies/sectors) or **entrepreneurship education**, and predominantly on **digital/
  web-based firms** (reflecting LS's Silicon Valley origin).
- **External validity gap:** applicability to **non-digital / physical / regulated** firms is
  under-studied; calls for **quantitative and longitudinal** research to establish effectiveness.
- **Doubts about scientific validity:** despite popularity, scholars question LS's scientific status
  given the limited empirical validation (pre-Camuffo-RCT critique; the RCTs sources 01/02 partially
  answer this).

## Relationship to other sources
Provides the *critique/limitations* balance: LS is popular but under-validated, especially outside
digital startups. The Camuffo RCTs (01/02) are the strongest answer to this gap; this SLR explains
why those RCTs were needed and why generalization beyond digital firms is still open.

## Reproducibility note
SLR synthesis; the "thin and digital-skewed evidence" conclusion is independently corroborated by
peer-reviewed SLRs (Frederiksen & Brem 2017; Ghezzi 2019), satisfying corroboration for this
load-bearing critique.
