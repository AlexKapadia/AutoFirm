## Core definitions (exact, corroborated across sources)
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** "the maximum revenue opportunity available if your product
  achieved full market penetration" — the total revenue if you sold to 100% of the market.
- **SAM (Serviceable Available Market):** "the specific segment of the market you can practically
  serve at this stage, based on your current resources, team size, capabilities, and business
  strategy" — the realistic portion of TAM you can target.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** the share of SAM you can realistically **capture in the
  near term (typically 1-3 years)**, given competition and go-to-market. (PAM, Product/Potential
  Addressable Market, is sometimes added as a wider future-expansion frame.)
- **Nesting:** SOM is subset of SAM is subset of TAM.

## Calculation methods (formulae, exact)
- **Bottom-up (preferred for credibility):**
  `Market Size = Annual Contract Value (ACV) x Number of Potential Customers`
  Builds from real unit economics + countable customer segments; "grounds your TAM, SAM, and SOM in
  how your business actually operates, making it far more credible to investors."
- **Top-down:** start from broad industry figures (Gartner, IBISWorld) and filter down by geography/
  segment/adoption assumptions. Faster but "often lacks specificity and can feel disconnected from
  your actual business."
- **Value-theory (third approach):** estimate TAM from the *value created for the customer* and the
  share of that value the product can capture (used when no clean comparable market exists).

## Key cautions (corroborated)
- **Top-down sizing inflates:** "almost all entrepreneurs claim a billion-dollar TAM," and such
  numbers are "chronically inflated" because they lean on vague top-down figures (Sramana Mitra,
  via the market-sizing literature). Bottom-up "is increasingly treated as the only version that
  really counts" by investors.
- **Best practice:** use top-down for vision/headroom, bottom-up for a credible path, and
  **cross-validate** the two. A common VC heuristic: capturing even 1-2% of a $50B TAM is a
  $500M-$1B opportunity — but only bottom-up SOM is credible for near-term planning.
