- **Authors:** Barton A. Weitz; Harish Sujan; Mita Sujan

## What the source actually says (faithful summary)
The paper provides the academic theory underneath ALL the practitioner methodologies (SPIN, Challenger, etc.): salesperson effectiveness is driven by **adaptive selling**.

- **Definition of adaptive selling:** "the altering of sales behaviors during a customer interaction or across customer interactions based on perceived information about the nature of the selling situation." (i.e., tailoring approach to the specific customer/situation rather than using one fixed pitch.)
- **The framework's drivers of adaptive behaviour:**
  - **Knowledge structures** — richer, better-organized **categories of customer types and matched sales strategies** let a salesperson recognize a situation and select the right approach. Experts have more elaborate, contingency-organized knowledge ("if customer is type X, use strategy Y").
  - **Motivation** — the salesperson must be *motivated* to practice adaptive (vs. routine) behaviour; intrinsic/learning-goal orientation supports it.
  - **Adaptive behaviour** then mediates **selling effectiveness/performance.**
- **ADAPTS scale (Spiro & Weitz 1990):** a 16-item self-report instrument measuring degree of practiced adaptive selling. Note (caveat the literature records): the original 16-item measure was later found NOT to be cleanly unidimensional as designed (including by Spiro & Weitz themselves), so shortened/refined versions exist.

## Key claims AutoFirm can rely on
1. **Selling effectiveness is driven by ADAPTING behaviour to the situation** — empirically the central academic construct of sales performance. Locator: Weitz/Sujan/Sujan 1986, framework.
2. Adaptation depends on **well-structured contingency knowledge ("if customer-type X → strategy Y")** — exactly the kind of rule base an AutoFirm agent can encode. Locator: knowledge-structures section.
3. Adaptive selling is a **measurable construct** (ADAPTS, 16 items) — giving AutoFirm a literature-grounded metric.

## Reproducibility note
The adaptive-selling definition and the knowledge/motivation/behaviour framework are stated in the 1986 JM abstract + framework (DOI above); the ADAPTS scale and its later unidimensionality critique are in Spiro & Weitz (1990, JMR) and subsequent psychometric re-evaluations.
