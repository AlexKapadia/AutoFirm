## What the source actually says (faithful summary)
Webster & Wind model organizational (industrial/institutional) buying as an **organizational decision-making process** involving "many persons, multiple goals, and potentially conflicting decision criteria" — fundamentally unlike individual B2C purchase.

- **The Buying Center / Decision-Making Unit (DMU):** all members of the organization who become involved in the buying process. Webster & Wind define **five buying roles** (later commonly extended to six):
  - **Users** — those who use the product/service.
  - **Influencers** — shape decision criteria / supply information (often technical).
  - **Buyers** — have formal authority to select the supplier and arrange terms (purchasing).
  - **Deciders** — have actual power to choose (may be informal).
  - **Gatekeepers** — control the flow of information into the buying center.
  - *(Common extension: **Initiator** — who first recognizes the need.)*
- **Four classes of variables** drive the decision: **Environmental, Organizational, Interpersonal (group), and Individual** determinants.
- **Task vs. non-task dimensions:** the *task* dimension = the specific buying problem to be solved (rational: price, quality, delivery); the *non-task* dimension = individual motives such as achievement and risk-reduction. BOTH operate — organizational buying is rational AND has individual/emotional drivers.
- **Buygrid (Robinson, Faris & Wind 1967):** the process runs through **eight BuyPhases** (problem/need recognition; general need description; product specification; supplier search; proposal solicitation; supplier selection; order-routine specification; performance review) across **three BuyClasses** — **New Task, Modified Rebuy, Straight Rebuy** — where effort/DMU size is largest for New Task and smallest for Straight Rebuy.
