## What the source actually says (faithful summary)
The authors review more than 250 journal articles and books to ask what is genuinely known about how advertising affects the consumer, and test the dominant hierarchy-of-effects assumption.

- **Central finding (the load-bearing claim):** there is "little support for any hierarchy, in the sense of temporal sequence, of effects." They find little evidence that consumers must pass through fixed cognitive→affective→behavioural stages in order.
- **Two flaws of persuasive-hierarchy models (the AIDA family):** (1) they assume the brain works through a series of stages "as if it were a primitive serial computer," when the brain actually works in parallel; (2) they ignore the consumer's experience of previous product usage.
- **Proposed alternative framework:** intermediate advertising effects should be modelled in a SPACE with three dimensions — **Cognition (C), Affect (A), and Experience (E)** — rather than as a fixed linear sequence. Different routes through C/A/E apply to different products/situations (the dimensions can combine in different orders).
