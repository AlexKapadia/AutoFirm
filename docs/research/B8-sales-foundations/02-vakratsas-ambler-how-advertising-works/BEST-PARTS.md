# BEST-PARTS — Vakratsas & Ambler (1999)

## ADOPT
- **Model buyer state as a multi-dimensional vector, not a single linear stage index.** AutoFirm's sales playbook should track each opportunity along (at least) cognition (does the buyer understand the problem/solution?), affect (preference/trust), and experience (prior usage/relationship) — mirroring the C/A/E space — *in addition to* the convenience funnel stage (source 01). *Build implication:* the L2.B8 opportunity record carries C/A/E signals (e.g. engagement depth, sentiment, prior-purchase flag) that drive next-best-action, instead of assuming a fixed stage order.
- **Adopt the empirical-humility principle as a hard rule.** Because the most-cited review in the field finds NO support for a fixed stage sequence, AutoFirm must never *gate* a deal purely on "it's only at stage 2 so it can't close." Stages inform reporting; evidence (engagement, qualification signals) drives action.

## REJECT
- **Reject any design that hard-codes strict sequential funnel progression as a model of the buyer.** Forcing buyers through ordered stages contradicts the strongest available evidence and would overfit to the linear-pipeline convention.

## Why this matters for generality
The C/A/E framing is product- and industry-agnostic (it was derived across 250+ studies spanning many categories), so it generalizes across the B12 industry panel far better than a single fixed funnel — a DTC impulse purchase (affect/experience-led) and an enterprise SaaS deal (cognition-led) are different *routes through the same space*, not different funnels.
