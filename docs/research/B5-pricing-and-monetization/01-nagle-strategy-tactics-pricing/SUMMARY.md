## Key claims (faithful)
1. **Three pricing orientations.** Pricing strategies fall into three families: **cost-based**
   (price = cost + target margin), **competition-based** (price set relative to competitors), and
   **value-based** (price set according to the customer's perceived/economic value). Nagle argues
   cost-plus is the most common but least profitable because cost does not determine what a
   customer will pay.
2. **Economic value != willingness to pay.** "There is a distinction between the economic value of
   a product and the customer's willingness to pay. Economic value is the potential financial
   benefit a product provides, while willingness to pay is influenced by perceived value." WTP is
   the realized, communicated subset of economic value.
3. **The value cascade / strategic pricing pyramid.** Profitable pricing proceeds in order:
   (i) create value, (ii) **communicate** value, (iii) set price structure/policy, (iv) set price
   level, (v) manage price (negotiation/realization). Price level sits near the *top*, not the
   foundation -- value creation and communication precede it.
4. **Segmentation is mandatory.** "All markets are made up of segments, where consumers are willing
   to pay varying amounts. Without creating prices tailored to each of these segments, your company
   will lose money." Value-based pricing requires fences/metrics that let segments self-select into
   different prices (links to second-degree price discrimination, source 03).
5. **Value communication.** A value-based price fails unless value is *understood*; price/value
   communication "protect[s] your value proposition from competitive encroachment, improve[s]
   willingness-to-pay, and increase[s] the likelihood of purchase."

## Reproducibility note
Claims 1-5 are the stable, repeatedly-restated theses of the text across editions and are
corroborated by the independent EVC framework (source 02), price-discrimination economics
(source 03), and Simon-Kucher's empirical pricing studies (source 12). A reviewer can re-derive
the orientation taxonomy and value cascade from any edition of the book or the Taylor & Francis
chapter above.
