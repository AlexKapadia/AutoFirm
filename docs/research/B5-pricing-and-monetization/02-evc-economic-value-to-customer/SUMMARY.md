# SUMMARY — Economic Value to the Customer (EVC)

  **Kent B. Monroe**, *Pricing: Making Profitable Decisions* (1990). Original EVC formulation

## Key claims and EXACT formula (faithful, in source notation)
1. **EVC definition (exact quote):** EVC "quantifies, in dollars, how much better off a customer is
   with your offering versus their next-best alternative (NBA)."
2. **EVC formula (exact, source notation):**
   ```
   EVC = Reference Value + Differentiation Value - Switching/Implementation Costs
   ```
   where **Differentiation Value** = positive differentiation value (cost reductions, revenue
   gains, working-capital and risk/compliance benefits) **minus** negative differentiation value
   (performance drawbacks / added costs vs. NBA).
3. **Component definitions (exact):**
   - **Reference Value** = "the price of the customer's next-best alternative (NBA) -- what they
     currently pay or would expect to pay for the competing solution."
   - **Differentiation Value** = "the incremental economic impact your offer delivers versus the
     NBA" (cost reductions: labor/scrap/energy; revenue gains: throughput/conversion/market timing;
     working-capital improvements; risk/compliance benefits).
   - **Switching/Implementation Costs** = "one-time or recurring expenses for training, integration,
     cutover downtime, and change management."
4. **Worked example (specialty chemicals, die-casting release agent), exact numbers from guide:**
   - NBA price: **$6.40/liter**; annual reference spend: **$21.5M**.
   - Monetized deltas ~**$3.48M** (scrap reduction $1.46M + cycle-time $0.22M + consumption savings
     $1.72M + tooling $0.081M).
   - First-year switching cost: **$170K**.
   - **First-year EVC ~ $24.81M; steady-state ~ $25.0M.**
5. **Pricing principle:** EVC is the *ceiling* on a rational customer's WTP; the value-based price
   is set as a fraction of the differentiation value (a "value-sharing" split between buyer and
   seller), with reference value as the floor anchor.
