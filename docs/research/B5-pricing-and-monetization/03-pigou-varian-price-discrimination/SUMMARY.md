## Key claims (faithful, in source notation)
1. **Pigou's three degrees (exact taxonomy):**
   - **First degree (perfect):** "the firm knows consumer preferences completely and can price
     separately to each consumer" -- charges each buyer's full WTP, extracting all consumer surplus.
     The theoretical maximum-profit benchmark; unattainable in practice (requires perfect info).
   - **Second degree (self-selection / nonlinear):** "consumers with different preferences choose
     to buy different goods at different prices (e.g., cell phone plans, health insurance contracts,
     types of gasoline)." Prices differ by *quantity/version* chosen; buyers sort themselves via
     menus. Underlies versioning, tiers, and two-part tariffs.
   - **Third degree (observable-segment):** "pricing based on limited observed characteristics,
     e.g. age, student status, gender ... The same quality is offered to all segments, but prices
     are different." Segment the market on observable signals of WTP.
2. **Varian's empirical generalization (exact):** "the most common form of price discrimination is
   likely third-degree price discrimination."
3. **Welfare/profit ordering:** first-degree extracts maximal surplus; second and third degree are
   feasible approximations that raise profit above uniform pricing when WTP is heterogeneous and
   arbitrage between segments can be prevented (the "fence" requirement).
4. **Two-part tariff** (special case of nonlinear/second-degree pricing): price = fixed access fee
   A + per-unit price p; under perfect info p = marginal cost and A = consumer surplus, extracting
   first-degree surplus. Basis for SaaS base-fee + usage models (source 11).
