## Key claims (faithful)
1. **Three pricing orientations, peer-reviewed restatement.** Hinterhuber (2004) frames pricing
   methods as **cost-based, competition-based, and value-based (customer-value-based)**, and argues
   value-based pricing yields superior profitability but is **under-adopted** because economic value
   is hard to quantify and communicate. (Independently corroborates source 01 claim 1.)
2. **Six-step value-based pricing methodology (faithful, the paper's framework).**
   (i) **Define pricing objectives**; (ii) **analyze the key elements of the pricing decision** -
   economic-value analysis, cost-volume-profit analysis, competitive analysis; (iii) **economic
   value analysis illustrated** (compute reference value + differentiation value); (iv) **manage
   economic value** (communicate it to customers); (v) **determine a range of profitable prices**;
   (vi) **implement price changes**. This is a peer-reviewed operationalization of the EVC ceiling
   into an actionable sequence - directly parallel to AutoFirm's pricing cascade.
3. **Economic value = reference value + differentiation value (Hinterhuber, faithful).** The paper
   builds economic value as the **reference value (next-best alternative price) plus differentiation
   value** (the monetized positive/negative performance differences), explicitly citing Nagle &
   Holden - a third independent statement of the EVC decomposition (sources 02, 13).
4. **Value-based pricing requires value quantification + communication (Hinterhuber 2008).** The
   2008 paper deepens the *value delivery* side: economic value must be measured AND communicated;
   absent communication, realized WTP falls short of economic value. (Corroborates source 01 claim 5.)
5. **Barriers are organizational, not just analytical.** Hinterhuber identifies the main obstacles to
   value-based pricing as **measurement difficulty, sales-force communication, and segmentation** -
   informing AutoFirm's emphasis on an explainable value-communication artifact and fences (source 03).
