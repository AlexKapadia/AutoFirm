## Key claims and EXACT numbers (faithful)
1. **Definition (price elasticity of demand):** e = (% change in quantity demanded) / (% change in
   price) = (dQ/Q) / (dP/P). For a linear/log demand curve, |e| > 1 = elastic (price cut raises
   revenue), |e| < 1 = inelastic (price rise raises revenue).
2. **Mean elasticity (exact):** Bijmolt et al. (2005) analyze **1,851 price elasticities from 81
   studies**; the **average price elasticity = -2.62.** (Tellis 1988 reported mean approx -1.76 /
   -2.5 depending on subset; the -2.62 figure is the 2005 update.)
3. **Determinants (directional generalizations):** elasticity magnitude varies systematically with
   brand, product category, stage of life cycle, and **research methodology** -- "empirical
   generalizations on price elasticity require modifications because of changes in market
   characteristics ... and changes in the research methodology used to assess price elasticities."
   (e.g., promotional/temporary price changes show larger elasticities than regular-price changes).
4. **Profit-maximizing markup rule (Lerner / inverse-elasticity, the operational consequence):**
   at the profit-maximizing price, (P - MC)/P = 1/|e|; i.e. optimal price P* = MC * |e|/(|e|-1) for
   constant-elasticity demand with |e| > 1. (Standard microeconomic result; the operational link
   from elasticity to price.)

## Reproducibility note
The -2.62 mean (n = 1,851 elasticities, 81 studies) is the headline result of Bijmolt et al. (2005),
reproducible from JMR 42(2) Table of results; corroborated directionally by Tellis (1988). The
Lerner inverse-elasticity rule is a standard reproducible microeconomic identity. Exact number +
locator recorded per DEPTH-RUBRIC sec 3.3.
