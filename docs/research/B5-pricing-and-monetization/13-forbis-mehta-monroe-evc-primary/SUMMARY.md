## Key claims (faithful)
1. **EVC concept (Forbis & Mehta 1981, faithful).** To persuade a customer to switch, a product must
   deliver at least as much **surplus value** beyond its price as the customer obtains from the
   incumbent ("reference") product. EVC = the price at which the new product offers the customer the
   **same net economic surplus** as the next-best alternative - i.e. the customer's WTP **ceiling**.
   The price gap up to EVC is split between the buyer ("incentive to purchase") and the seller.
2. **EVC structure (Forbis & Mehta, faithful).** EVC is built from two parts measured against the
   reference product: (a) **start-up costs** the new product changes (installation, training,
   inventory, etc.) and (b) **post-purchase / life-cycle costs** it changes (operating, maintenance,
   downtime, output value). The sum of the customer's net economic gains over the reference product,
   added to the reference price, gives EVC. This is the same decomposition source 02 states as
   EVC = Reference Value + Differentiation Value - Switching/Implementation Costs.
3. **Value-based price < EVC (Forbis & Mehta).** A rational customer never pays more than EVC; the
   seller prices **below** EVC by enough to give the customer a purchase incentive - the
   "value-sharing" split. Reference value is the floor anchor; EVC is the ceiling.
4. **Perceived value & the price-value trade-off (Monroe).** Monroe's behavioral-pricing work
   establishes that buyers judge price against **perceived value** and a **reference price**; value
   = perceived benefits relative to perceived sacrifice (price). This independently grounds the
   "reference value" term in EVC and links to behavioral framing (source 09).
5. **Industrial focus & generality.** Forbis & Mehta develop EVC for *industrial* (B2B) products
   where economic benefits are quantifiable - exactly AutoFirm's B2B EVC use case - while the
   surplus-value logic generalizes to any setting with a definable next-best alternative.
