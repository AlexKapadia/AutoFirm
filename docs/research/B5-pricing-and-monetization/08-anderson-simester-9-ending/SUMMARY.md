# SUMMARY — Effects of $9 Price Endings on Retail Sales (Anderson & Simester)

## Full citation
- **Primary:** Anderson, E. T. & Simester, D. I. (2003). "Effects of $9 Price Endings on Retail
  Sales: Evidence from Field Experiments." *Quantitative Marketing and Economics* 1(1), 93-110.
  https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225849046
- **Corroborating (mechanism / left-digit):** Thomas, M. & Morwitz, V. (2005). "Penny Wise and Pound
  Foolish: The Left-Digit Effect in Price Cognition." *Journal of Consumer Research* 32(1), 54-64.
- **Corroborating (UK field study):** "The Effect of 9-Ending Prices on Retail Sales: A Quantitative
  UK Based Field Study." https://www.researchgate.net/publication/233627869
- **Contradicting / boundary (down-rate):** Macé, S. et al. (2022). "Psychological price perception
  may exert a weaker effect ... fail to reproduce either a left-digit or perceptual-fluency effect."
  *PLOS ONE* 17(7): e0270850. https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0270850
- **Year:** 2003 (primary).
- **Venue:** *Quantitative Marketing and Economics* (peer-reviewed).

## Ontology question(s) informed
L1.B5.1 (behavioral / psychological pricing tactics affecting WTP). Feeds L2.B5 price-presentation.

## GRADE tier
**Moderate-High** for the existence of a 9-ending effect (field experiments by Anderson & Simester,
corroborated by Thomas & Morwitz and a UK field study). **Down-rated** on *magnitude/universality*
because a large pre-registered PLOS ONE study (2022) failed to reproduce the left-digit/fluency
effect online -- the effect is real in some contexts but context-dependent and not universal.

## Key claims and EXACT findings (faithful)
1. **Field-experiment finding (exact):** Anderson & Simester ran field experiments with a catalog
   retailer; raising a price from a round ending to a **$9-ending** (e.g. $34 -> $39) *increased*
   demand, and **9-endings increased sales even when the price was higher** than a lower
   non-9-ending price -- evidence that $9 endings act as a low-price *signal*, not just a small
   discount.
2. **Two mechanisms (exact):** (a) **Left-digit / underestimation ("level effect")** -- consumers
   anchor on the leftmost digit, so $1.99 is encoded closer to $1 than $2; and (b) a prospect-theory
   **"image effect"** -- 9-endings are associated with sales/bargains. Thomas & Morwitz (2005)
   provide the cognitive left-digit evidence.
3. **Prevalence (exact range):** estimates that **9-endings account for between 30% and 95% of
   retail prices** (varies by study/category).
4. **Boundary condition (exact):** the PLOS ONE (2022) pre-registered online experiment "fail[ed] to
   reproduce either a left-digit or perceptual-fluency effect," indicating the effect is **weaker /
   context-dependent online** and not a guaranteed lever.

## Reproducibility note
The directional field-experiment result (9-endings raise demand, sometimes even at higher absolute
price) is reproducible from Anderson & Simester (2003) and the UK field study; the left-digit
cognitive mechanism from Thomas & Morwitz (2005). The PLOS ONE null is recorded as the explicit
down-rate/boundary per DEPTH-RUBRIC sec 2 (inconsistency across sources). Three+ independent sources,
including a contradicting one -> honest evidence body, not cherry-picked.
