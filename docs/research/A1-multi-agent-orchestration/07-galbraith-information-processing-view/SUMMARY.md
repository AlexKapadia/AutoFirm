## Key claims (faithful)

### Core proposition (load-bearing)
- "the greater the **uncertainty** of the task, the greater the **amount of information** that has to
  be processed between decision makers during the execution of the task" (to achieve a given
  performance level). Rooted in Herbert Simon's cognitive-limits theory.

### Two routes when information-processing demand exceeds capacity
An organization facing rising uncertainty must either **reduce the need to process information** or
**increase its capacity to process information**. Galbraith's four design strategies:
1. **Reduce need — Creation of slack resources** (e.g. buffers, longer lead times) so fewer
   exceptions must be coordinated.
2. **Reduce need — Creation of self-contained tasks** (group around outputs so a unit has the
   resources to act without cross-unit coordination).
3. **Increase capacity — Investment in vertical information systems** (formal channels/IT that move
   information up the hierarchy faster).
4. **Increase capacity — Creation of lateral relations** (direct cross-unit contact, liaison roles,
   task forces, teams) that move decision-making to where the information is.

### Default mechanisms before strategies
Routine/low-uncertainty tasks are coordinated by **rules/programs** and **hierarchy** (referral of
exceptions upward); these channels overload as uncertainty grows, forcing one of the four strategies.
