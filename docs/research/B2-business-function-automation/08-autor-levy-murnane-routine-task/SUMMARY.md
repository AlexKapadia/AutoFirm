- **L1.B2.3** Which functions automate — the FOUNDATIONAL theory (the "routine vs. non-routine" task model that all later work builds on).

## GRADE tier
- **High** — peer-reviewed in a top-5 economics journal (QJE), the seminal task-based framework underpinning Frey-Osborne, MGI, and Arntz. Foundational and heavily replicated.

## Key claims (faithful to source)

**The task model of computerization.** Computer capital:
1. **Substitutes** for workers in carrying out a limited and well-defined set of cognitive and
   manual activities — those that can be accomplished by **following explicit rules** ("routine"
   tasks).
2. **Complements** workers in carrying out **non-routine problem-solving and complex communication**
   tasks.

**The 2×2 task taxonomy** (the durable contribution):
- **Routine cognitive** (e.g. bookkeeping, repetitive calculation) — substitutable.
- **Routine manual** (e.g. repetitive assembly) — substitutable.
- **Non-routine cognitive / analytic & interactive** (problem-solving, complex communication) — complemented.
- **Non-routine manual** (e.g. driving, flexible physical work) — historically resistant.

**Empirical finding (exact):** within industries, occupations, and education groups,
computerization is associated with **reduced labour input of routine manual and routine cognitive
tasks and increased labour input of non-routine cognitive tasks.** The model explains **about 60
percent** of the estimated relative demand shift favouring college-educated labour over 1970–1998.

## Reproducibility note
The substitute-routine / complement-nonroutine dichotomy, the 2×2 routine×(cognitive/manual) taxonomy, and the "explains ~60% of the 1970-1998 college demand shift" figure are reproduced from Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003), QJE 118(4). Confirmable in the QJE published article.
