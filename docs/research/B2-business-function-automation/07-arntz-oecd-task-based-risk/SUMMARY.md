## Questions informed
- **L1.B2.3** Which functions automate, and to what degree — the critical *task-based* corrective to Frey-Osborne.

## GRADE tier
- **High** — OECD working paper, methodologically rigorous comparative analysis across 21 OECD countries using PIAAC micro-data; the canonical counterpoint to Frey-Osborne and the source of the "tasks not jobs" correction. (Working paper, not journal; but OECD primary statistics + transparent method → High for this claim.)

## Key claims (faithful to source, exact numbers)

**Method.** A **task-based** approach using individual-level **PIAAC** (Programme for the
International Assessment of Adult Competencies) data, which captures the *actual task content of
each worker's job* — explicitly correcting Frey-Osborne's assumption that whole occupations
automate. Workers in the same occupation differ in tasks, so occupation-level estimates overstate.

**Headline finding (exact):** On average across 21 OECD countries, **only 9% of jobs are highly
automatable** (probability of automation > 0.7) — versus the **47%** that the occupation-based
(Frey-Osborne) approach yields when applied to the same workers. For the US specifically, ~9%
(range across countries: ~6% in Korea/Estonia to ~12% in Austria/Germany).

**Key mechanism:** "previous studies... assume whole occupations rather than single job-tasks are
automated by technology, which might lead to an overestimation of job automatibility." Many
workers in "automatable" occupations actually perform many *non-automatable* tasks.

**Caveat stated:** technical possibility ≠ actual automation — adoption is hindered by legal,
ethical, and economic obstacles, and by worker adaptation (task reallocation within jobs).

## Reproducibility note
The 9% (task-based, >0.7) vs. 47% (occupation-based) contrast and the PIAAC-based method are reproduced from Arntz/Gregory/Zierahn (2016) OECD WP No. 189, §§3–5 and the headline abstract. Confirmable in the OECD PDF.
