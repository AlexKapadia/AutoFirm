# SUMMARY — Binet & Field: The Long and the Short of It (Brand vs. Performance)

**Question(s) informed:** L1.B7.1 (brand-building vs. performance/activation; the budget-split evidence).

## Full citation
- **Title:** The Long and the Short of It: Balancing Short and Long-Term Marketing Strategies
- **Authors:** Les Binet & Peter Field
- **Year:** 2013
- **Venue/Publisher:** Institute of Practitioners in Advertising (IPA), London. ISBN 978-0-85294-134-8.
- **Related primary works:** Binet & Field (2007) *Marketing in the Era of Accountability* (IPA); Field (2019) *The Crisis in Creative Effectiveness* (IPA); B2B extension: *The Five Principles of Growth in B2B Marketing* (2019, LinkedIn B2B Institute x Binet/Field).
- **URL (pointer):** https://ipa.co.uk/knowledge/effectiveness-research-analysis/les-binet-peter-field

## GRADE tier
**Moderate.** Rigorous, large-N analysis of the **IPA Effectiveness Databank (996 campaigns, 1980-2010)** by recognized industry primary researchers, peer-reviewed within the IPA awards process. Tiered Moderate (industry primary data, not an academic-journal RCT) and corroborated by independent practitioner analyses and the Ehrenberg-Bass evidence (folder 09) on the role of brand-building reach. Down-rated slightly for self-reported case-study data (selection toward award entrants); up-rated for large N, consistency, and independent replication of the directional finding.

## Core content (faithful summary)
Analyzing ~1,000 campaigns, Binet & Field distinguish **two mechanisms** of advertising:
1. **Brand building** (long-term): broad-reach, emotional, brand-led communication. Effects **build slowly and decay slowly**; drives sustained sales/profit, pricing power, reduced price sensitivity, and lower long-run CAC. Most efficient growth at scale.
2. **Sales activation** (short-term / performance): targeted, rational, response-driven (promotions, performance/direct ads). Effects are **immediate but decay fast** (weeks-months); efficient at converting in-market demand, poor at long-run base growth.

**Headline finding — the ~60:40 rule.** Across the databank, the budget split that maximizes long-run effectiveness/profit averages **~60% brand-building : ~40% sales activation** (it varies by category; B2B and considered purchases skew differently). Brands systematically **over-invest in short-term activation and under-invest in brand**, especially as digital made activation easy to measure.

## Key claims (locators)
1. Optimal long-run budget split averages **~60% brand / ~40% activation** (Binet & Field 2013, IPA; databank of 996 cases 1980-2010).
2. Brand-building effects decay over **years**; activation effects decay over **weeks/months** — different time signatures require different measurement windows.
3. Over-reliance on short-term metrics (last-click ROI) drives chronic under-investment in brand and erodes long-term growth ("short-termism").

## Reproducibility note
The 60:40 figure is an **average across categories**, not a universal constant — Binet/Field explicitly vary it (e.g. closer to 50:50 or even activation-heavier for some B2B/online-only/considered-purchase contexts). Directional finding (long-term brand effects exist, decay slowly, and are systematically under-funded) is independently corroborated.
