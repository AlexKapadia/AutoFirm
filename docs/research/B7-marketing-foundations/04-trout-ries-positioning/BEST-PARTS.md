# BEST-PARTS — Trout & Ries Positioning

## ADOPT
- **Positioning = a perceptual claim relative to competitors, expressed as ONE ownable attribute.** **Build implication:** the positioning stage emits a structured `PositioningStatement` { target_segment, category_frame, single_key_attribute, competitors_referenced, point_of_difference }. Enforcing a *single* key attribute is a guardrail against the "be everything" failure mode.
- **Category-leadership logic as a strategy selector.** Given a competitive map (from L1.B3 opportunity validation), the engine picks: first-in-category / create-new-category / against-the-leader. **Build implication:** deterministic decision rule keyed on the client's competitive rank, explainable per CLAUDE.md 3.11.
- **Focus principle as a test.** **Build implication:** an adversarial QA test rejects positioning outputs that claim >1 primary attribute or fail to name the competitive frame — keeps outputs disciplined and falsifiable.

## REJECT
- Reject Ries/Trout's later anti-line-extension absolutism as a hard rule — it is contested by Ehrenberg-Bass evidence (folder 09) that brand growth comes from penetration/availability, not narrow attribute-ownership. **Treat positioning focus as a messaging-clarity heuristic, not a growth law.** This conflict is resolved in SYNTHESIS (positioning governs *message*; availability/penetration governs *growth*).

## Why
Positioning gives the playbook a crisp, testable messaging contract and a competitive-strategy selector, while flagging the genuine tension with modern growth science so AutoFirm does not overfit to one school.
