# SUMMARY — Trout & Ries: Positioning

**Question(s) informed:** L1.B7.1 (the "P" in STP — positioning theory, primary origin).

## Full citations
- **Title:** "Positioning" Is a Game People Play in Today's Me-Too Marketplace — **Author:** Jack Trout — **Year:** 1969 — **Venue:** *Industrial Marketing*, Vol. 54, No. 6, pp. 51-55. (The article that introduced "positioning" to marketing.)
- **Title:** Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind — **Authors:** Al Ries & Jack Trout — **Year:** 1981 (orig. as a 1972 *Advertising Age* series "The Positioning Era") — **Venue:** McGraw-Hill (book; primary popularization).
- Attribution corroborated: martechsquare "Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning — The Greatest Marketing Idea of 1969" and Umbrex STP framework.

## GRADE tier
**Low->Moderate.** Trade-press article + practitioner book (not peer-reviewed), so tier is bounded; but they are the **primary origin** of the positioning concept and are the citation of record. Component claims corroborated by Kotler (folder 03) and independent framework summaries. Used for the *concept and definition*, not for quantitative effect sizes.

## Core content (faithful summary)
Positioning is **not what you do to a product — it is what you do to the mind of the prospect.** You position the product in the mind of the target customer. Core theses:
1. The mind is **overloaded** with communications; it defends itself by **simplifying and ranking** — it holds a small "ladder" of brands per category. Successful positioning occupies a clear rung.
2. Positioning is **relative**: defined against competitors already in the mind, not in absolute terms. The strongest position is being **first** in a category; if not first, **create a new category** you can be first in, or position *against* the leader (e.g. "the No. 2 that tries harder").
3. **Less is more / focus:** a brand should own *one* word/attribute in the mind; trying to be everything dilutes the position (line-extension trap).

## Key claims (locators)
1. "Positioning starts with a product... But positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect." (Ries & Trout, 1981, Ch. 1.)
2. Consumers rank brands on mental "ladders"; share of mind tends to track position on the ladder.
3. Strategic options when not the category leader: be-first-in-a-new-category; reposition the competitor; against-the-leader positioning.

## Reproducibility note
Definition and ladder/leadership theses reproducible from *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981), Ch. 1-4. Origin date (1969 *Industrial Marketing* article by Trout) corroborated by multiple independent histories.
