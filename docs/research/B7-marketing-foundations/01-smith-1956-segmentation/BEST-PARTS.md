# BEST-PARTS — Smith (1956)

## ADOPT
- **Heterogeneity-first premise.** AutoFirm's marketing playbook (L2.B7) must *begin* by testing whether a client's market is heterogeneous enough to segment, vs. better served by differentiation of one offering. This is a decision node, not an assumption. **Build implication:** the marketing-playbook engine takes a `market_heterogeneity` signal (from L1.B4.2 customer modeling / segmentation analysis) and branches: segment vs. mass-differentiate.
- **Segmentation = disaggregation into homogeneous sub-markets, each with its own mix.** This is the contract for the segmentation stage: output is a set of segments, each carrying its own downstream 4Ps parameters. **Build implication:** typed `Segment` contract { id, defining_variables, size_estimate, distinct_needs } feeding per-segment mix decisions.

## REJECT / scope-limit
- **Do NOT treat differentiation and segmentation as mutually exclusive in execution.** Smith presents them as analytically distinct *strategies*, but modern practice (and AutoFirm) routinely sequences them (segment, then differentiate within a segment). Adopt the distinction as a decision lens, not a hard either/or.
- Smith predates digital/behavioral segmentation; do not rely on it for *which variables* to segment on (use modern segmentation evidence — geographic/demographic/psychographic/behavioral — corroborated in folder 03).

## Why
Anchoring the playbook on the original, peer-reviewed definition keeps AutoFirm's segmentation step general (any industry) and defensible (cited to the field's origin), satisfying the "general, not overfit" bar (CLAUDE.md 3.9).
