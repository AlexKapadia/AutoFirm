## Core thesis (faithful summary)
Smith frames **product differentiation** and **market segmentation** as two *alternative* strategic responses to the same fact: real markets are **heterogeneous**, not the homogeneous markets assumed by perfect-competition theory.
- **Product differentiation:** the firm bends *demand* toward its supply — using promotion/advertising to make a broad market accept a relatively standardized offering as distinctive.
- **Market segmentation:** the firm bends *supply* toward heterogeneous demand — viewing the market as several smaller homogeneous sub-markets defined by differing preferences, tailoring products/messaging to those segments.

Key conceptual move: heterogeneity of demand is the *rule*; segmentation is the "rational and more precise adjustment of product and marketing effort to consumer or user requirements."

## Key claims (with locators)
1. Markets are characterized by **diversity/heterogeneity** of demand; classical homogeneity is the exception (pp. 3-4).
2. **Segmentation = disaggregation** of a heterogeneous market into smaller, homogeneous sub-markets, each addressed with a tailored marketing mix (pp. 5-6).
3. **Differentiation = aggregation**: shaping a broad market's perception of a standardized product via promotion (pp. 4-5).
4. The choice is a strategic, resource-dependent decision; segmentation fits when demand is genuinely heterogeneous and sub-markets are profitably serveable.
