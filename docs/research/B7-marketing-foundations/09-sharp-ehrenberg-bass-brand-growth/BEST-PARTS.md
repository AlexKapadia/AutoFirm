# BEST-PARTS — Sharp / Ehrenberg-Bass

## ADOPT
- **Penetration-first growth doctrine.** AutoFirm's marketing playbook should default growth objectives to **acquiring new/light buyers (penetration)**, not loyalty programs, unless evidence for a specific client says otherwise. **Build implication:** the objective-setting stage defaults the growth KPI to penetration/reach; loyalty tactics are secondary and must justify themselves.
- **Mental + physical availability as the two growth levers.** **Build implication:** maps cleanly onto AutoFirm's stack — mental availability -> brand/advertising + distinctive-asset stage; physical availability -> Place/distribution (4Ps, folder 02; ops L1.B11.1). Gives a principled, evidence-backed definition of what brand spend is buying.
- **Distinctive brand assets** as a measurable deliverable (logos, colors, characters, taglines that trigger brand recall). **Build implication:** brand stage tracks distinctive-asset uniqueness/fame as a metric for the evidence showcase.
- **Reach-broad default** for growth campaigns (vs. hyper-narrow targeting), tempered by budget reality.

## REJECT
- **Reject the strongest anti-segmentation / anti-differentiation reading.** Ehrenberg-Bass is empirically powerful for *growth of established FMCG-style brands*, but AutoFirm serves the full industry panel incl. B2B SaaS, niche professional services, and regulated fintech/health where targeted segmentation (STP, folders 01/03) genuinely matters. **Resolution (see SYNTHESIS):** use availability/penetration as the growth ENGINE; use STP/positioning to decide REACH BOUNDARIES and MESSAGE — they are complementary, not contradictory.

## Why
Provides the empirical, replicated science of growth (the strongest evidence base in the field) and a testable definition of brand spend's job — while its scope limits keep AutoFirm from overfitting FMCG laws to every industry.
