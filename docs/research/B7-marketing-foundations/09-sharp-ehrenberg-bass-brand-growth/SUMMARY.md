# SUMMARY — Sharp / Ehrenberg-Bass: Empirical Laws of Brand Growth

**Question(s) informed:** L1.B7.1 (brand vs. performance; the empirical-science view of how brands actually grow).

## Core content (faithful summary)
The Ehrenberg-Bass "empirical generalizations" school argues brand growth follows replicated **laws**, not bespoke strategy:

1. **Double Jeopardy Law.** Brands with smaller market share have **both** fewer buyers **and** slightly lower loyalty (purchase frequency); large brands have more buyers and marginally higher loyalty. Loyalty is largely a *function of penetration*, not an independent lever. -> **Growth comes overwhelmingly from increasing PENETRATION (more buyers), not from increasing loyalty of existing buyers.**
2. **Mental & Physical Availability.** Brands grow by being **easy to think of** (mental availability — built via distinctive brand assets and broad-reach advertising) and **easy to buy** (physical availability — distribution/Place). Marketing's job is to refresh and extend memory structures across many "Category Entry Points."
3. **Mass reach > narrow targeting for growth.** Because growth = acquiring many light/non-buyers, broad reach (not narrow segmentation) drives penetration. Most buyers are **light buyers** (Pareto is ~60/40, not 80/20).
4. **Distinctive assets, not differentiation.** Brands compete on being noticed and remembered (distinctiveness), more than on rational differentiation.

## Key claims (locators)
1. Loyalty metrics are predictable from penetration via the Double Jeopardy pattern (Ehrenberg et al. 1990, *JoM* 54(3)).
2. Buying behavior across brands fits the **NBD-Dirichlet** model; brand performance measures are benchmarkable (Uncles et al. 2004).
3. Growth is driven by penetration via mental + physical availability (Sharp 2010, Ch. on growth).

## Reproducibility note
Double Jeopardy and Dirichlet patterns are among the most-replicated empirical generalizations in marketing (hundreds of categories). Reproducible from panel purchase data. Some claims (e.g. the strong anti-differentiation stance) are contested vs. positioning theory (folder 04) — noted, not hidden.
