## GRADE tier
**High.** Peer-reviewed top economics journal, large panel dataset (300+ large US firms), repeated
over time. Up-rated for large, consistent, longitudinal effect. The dataset is US large-firm
specific (indirectness caveat noted below).

## Key findings (EXACT figures)
Dataset: detailed managerial job descriptions and reporting relationships for **300+ large U.S.
firms** (averaging ~50,000 employees), panel from **1986 to ~1999/2003**.

1. **CEO span of control widened:** the number of managers reporting directly to the CEO rose from
   an **average of ~4 (1986) to ~7** (end of period).
2. **Hierarchy got shorter:** the number of levels between the **division heads and the CEO declined
   by ~25%**.
3. **Division heads moved closer to the top:** the number of division heads reporting directly to
   the CEO **increased by ~300%**.
4. **COO decline:** a notable decline in Chief Operating Officer positions accounted for a
   substantial part of the increased CEO span.
5. **Compensation followed structure:** managers who moved closer to the CEO received higher pay and
   greater long-term incentives — reflecting genuine **delegation of authority**, not just
   chart cosmetics.

Interpretation: firms flattened as **information technology disseminated knowledge**, letting
lower-level managers handle more decisions — an organizational response to technological change
(consistent with Galbraith OIPT, source 01: better vertical information systems raise capacity).

## Reproducibility note
The four headline figures (4->7 direct reports; -25% levels; +300% division heads to CEO; COO
decline) are reproduced identically across the NBER digest, the NBER working paper, and the RESt
published version. A reviewer can re-derive from the open NBER digest/working paper.
