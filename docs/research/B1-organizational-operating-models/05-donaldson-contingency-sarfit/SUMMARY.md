## Definition & core claim
**Contingency theory:** there is no single best way to organize; the optimal structure is
*contingent* on the organization's situation (its contingency variables). Effectiveness depends on
**fit** between structure and contingencies. Primary contingencies (from Burns & Stalker 1961,
Lawrence & Lorsch 1967, Thompson 1967, and the Aston studies): **size, strategy/diversification,
task uncertainty (technology), and environment (uncertainty/stability).**

## The SARFIT model (Structural Adaptation to Regain Fit)
Mechanism (exact logic):
1. An organization is initially **in fit** — its structure fits its current contingency level.
2. **Fit positively affects performance.**
3. The organization **changes its contingency level** (e.g. grows, diversifies) while keeping its
   existing structure -> the structure becomes a **misfit** with the new contingency.
4. **Misfit lowers performance.**
5. Low performance **provokes structural adaptation** -> a new structure that fits -> performance
   recovers -> further expansion -> misfit again. The cycle repeats.

Key insight: **fit and misfit are temporary, alternating states.** SARFIT answers the critique that
contingency theory is "static" — it is an explicit *dynamic* theory of organizational change.

## Reproducibility note
SARFIT's five-step cycle and the "fit affects performance; contingency change -> misfit -> adapt"
chain are reproduced consistently across the Springer chapter, the 1987 JMS article, and Donaldson
(2001). A reviewer can re-derive from any one.
