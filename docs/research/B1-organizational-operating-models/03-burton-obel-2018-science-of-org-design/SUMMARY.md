## Central thesis (exact framing)
The fundamental organizational design question is **how to create a FIT between STRUCTURE and
COORDINATION**, where:
- **Structure** *"breaks a big purpose or problem into smaller problems and units"* (division of
  labour / who reports to whom / how work is grouped), and
- **Coordination** *"manages these smaller problems, units, and tasks into a whole so that they fit
  together to achieve an overall purpose."*

Design should be **scientific and forward-looking** (not merely descriptive of existing org charts).

## Key constructs
- **Information-processing view (inherited from Galbraith, source 01):** organizations face
  information-processing demands and must either reduce the need (via structure/task design) or
  increase capacity (via coordination mechanisms).
- **Multi-contingency model** — design fit depends on multiple interacting contingencies:
  environmental uncertainty & complexity, task interdependence, strategy & goals, technology / work
  processes, and human capital / capabilities. (No single contingency determines design.)
- **Misfit costs:** when structure and coordination contradict each other, performance falls — a
  *cost* is incurred. Achieving structure<->coordination alignment yields higher performance than a
  mismatch. (Design propositions link each contingency configuration to an optimal design.)
