## Central thesis (exact)
Abstract (verbatim): *"The purpose of this paper is to explain why task uncertainty is related to
organizational form. In so doing the cognitive limits theory of Herbert Simon was the guiding
influence. As the consequences of cognitive limits were traced through the framework various
organization design strategies were articulated."*

Core proposition: **the greater the task uncertainty, the greater the amount of information that
must be processed among decision makers during execution to achieve a given level of performance.**
Organizations facing high uncertainty must either (a) **reduce the need** to process information, or
(b) **increase the capacity** to process information.

## The four design strategies (verbatim labels)
**Reduce the need for information processing**
1. **Creation of slack resources** — lower required performance (longer lead times, larger
   buffers/inventory) so fewer exceptions must be coordinated.
2. **Creation of self-contained tasks** — restructure around outputs (product/geography/customer)
   so each unit holds the resources it needs, cutting cross-unit interdependence and coordination.

**Increase the capacity to process information**
3. **Investment in vertical information systems** — formal systems moving information up the
   hierarchy to support apex decision-making (planning, scheduling, MIS).
4. **Creation of lateral relations** — cross-functional coordination *across* the hierarchy
   (liaison roles, task forces, teams, integrating roles, matrix) that pushes decisions down to
   where the information already is, instead of overloading the vertical channel.

Galbraith's managerial claim: these four are **alternatives** — an organization that does not
consciously choose one falls back by default on slack resources or self-contained tasks (the costly
defaults).
