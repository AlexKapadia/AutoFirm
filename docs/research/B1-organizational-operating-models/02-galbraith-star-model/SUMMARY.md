## The five design policy areas (the points of the Star)
1. **Strategy** — direction: vision, mission, values, goals. **Comes first** because it sets the
   criteria for trade-offs in the other four. ("Strategy drives Structure, Processes, Rewards,
   People.")
2. **Structure** — where formal power and authority sit; grouping of people and work. Five primary
   structure types: **Functional, Geographic, Product, Customer, Front-back hybrid.** Structure
   choices: specialization, shape (span/levels), distribution of power, departmentalization.
3. **Processes** — verbatim: a *"series of connected activities that move information up, down, and
   across the organization."* Includes **lateral connections**: networks, teams, integrative roles,
   matrix structures (the embodiment of Galbraith 1974's "lateral relations").
4. **Rewards** — metrics, desired behaviours, compensation, recognition; aligns individual goals to
   organizational goals.
5. **People** — HR policies for selection, performance management, training, development; builds the
   skills/mindsets the design requires.

## Core principle (alignment / fit)
Verbatim (reference doc): *"Alignment among the five policy areas of the Star Model leads to desired
behaviors that shape the organization's culture and drive performance."* When misaligned,
organizations experience negative team behaviours, declining culture, and performance drops
("shadow organizations" / friction appear). Structure is **only one** of five levers — designing
structure alone is the classic failure mode.

## Reproducibility note
The five points and the "strategy-first, then align the other four" logic are consistently
reproduced across the originator's own PDF and independent practitioner explanations, and are the
explicit basis of Burton & Obel's structure<->coordination fit (source 03).
