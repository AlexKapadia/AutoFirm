# The Social Psychology of Organizations - Role Theory & the Role Episode Model

**Question informed:** L1.A1.5 (role-spec authoring, role clarity, onboarding); feeds L2.ORG, L2.A2.

## Full citation
- **Title:** The Social Psychology of Organizations (esp. the role-episode / role-set model)
- **Authors:** Daniel Katz; Robert L. Kahn
- **Year:** 1st ed. 1966; 2nd ed. 1978 (Wiley). Role-episode model is from the 1978 edition.
- **Venue:** John Wiley & Sons (foundational org-behaviour text - primary).
- **GRADE tier:** **High** - the canonical primary statement of organizational role theory;
  cited across org behaviour, sociology, and management for decades.
- **Corroborating:** Biddle, B.J. (1986), "Recent Developments in Role Theory," *Annual Review
  of Sociology*, 12, 67-92, https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.so.12.080186.000435.

## What it is
The foundational theory of **organizational roles**: how a position's behaviour is defined and
transmitted by the **role set** (the people who depend on and interact with the role), via an
iterative **role episode** of sending and receiving expectations until enacted behaviour matches
expectations.

## Key claims (exact)
**Three forms of a role** (standard role-theory taxonomy):
1. **Expected role** - what the role set expects of the incumbent.
2. **Perceived role** - how the incumbent thinks they should behave to meet expectations.
3. **Enacted role** - how the incumbent actually behaves.

**The Role Episode Model (Katz & Kahn, 1978).** A cyclic, interactional process between the
**role senders** (the role set) and the **focal person** (incumbent):
- **Role expectations** (held by senders) -> **sent role** (the expectations communicated) ->
  **received role** (the incumbent's perception) -> **role behaviour** (enactment) -> which the
  senders observe and adjust their expectations, closing the loop.
- "The role-episode review process is a dynamic one, whereby role-sending and role-receiving
  continue until the perception of role-enactment conforms to the role-expectations."

**Role stress constructs:** **role ambiguity** (incumbent lacks clear information about
expectations) and **role conflict** (incompatible expectations) - both degrade performance and
wellbeing; clear role-sending reduces them.

**Roles give organisations stability:** "people may enter and quit but organizations remain
intact because of [the] roles that guide expected behaviors" - roles are the durable structure,
independent of any one incumbent.

## Up/down-rate reasoning
High tier: primary foundational text; corroborated by Biddle's *Annual Review of Sociology*
synthesis. Claims used are definitional (the three role forms, the episode loop, ambiguity vs.
conflict) - not contested.

## Reproducibility note
The role-episode model and the expected/perceived/enacted triad are reproduced in any role-theory
review (Biddle 1986; standard OB texts). A reviewer can verify against Katz & Kahn (1978, ch. on
role theory) or Biddle (1986).
