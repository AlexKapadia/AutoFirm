# Motivation through the Design of Work: the Job Characteristics Model

**Question informed:** L1.A1.5 (manager-authored role-spec / role-charter design); feeds L2.ORG.

## Full citation
- **Title:** Motivation through the design of work: Test of a theory (the Job Characteristics
  Model / JCM); core theory also in *Work Redesign* (Hackman & Oldham, 1980).
- **Authors:** J. Richard Hackman; Greg R. Oldham
- **Year:** 1976 (Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 16(2), 250-279); JDS instrument
  1975 (Journal of Applied Psychology, 60(2), 159-170); *Work Redesign*, Addison-Wesley, 1980.
- **Venue:** Organizational Behavior and Human Performance (peer-reviewed); Addison-Wesley (book).
- **URL/DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0030-5073(76)90016-7 (1976 article);
  https://doi.org/10.1037/h0076546 (1975 JDS).
- **Empirical base:** developed/validated on **658 workers across 62 jobs in 7 organisations**.
- **GRADE tier:** **High** - foundational peer-reviewed theory in work/org psychology, replicated
  for 45+ years; one of the most-cited job-design frameworks.

## What it is
A theory of how the **design of a role** drives motivation, performance, and retention. It names
the five **core job characteristics** an institution should engineer into any role, the three
**critical psychological states** they produce, and the personal/work **outcomes**.

## Key claims (exact)
**Five core job dimensions:**
1. **Skill variety** - range of skills/activities the role requires.
2. **Task identity** - degree to which the role completes a whole, identifiable piece of work.
3. **Task significance** - impact of the work on others / the larger system.
4. **Autonomy** - freedom/discretion in scheduling and method.
5. **Feedback** - degree to which doing the work yields direct, clear information on performance.

**Three critical psychological states:** experienced **meaningfulness** of the work (from variety,
identity, significance), experienced **responsibility** for outcomes (from autonomy), and
**knowledge of results** (from feedback).

**The Motivating Potential Score (MPS) - reproduced exactly:**

    MPS = [ (Skill Variety + Task Identity + Task Significance) / 3 ] x Autonomy x Feedback

Note the **multiplicative** form for Autonomy and Feedback: if either is ~0, MPS collapses
regardless of the other dimensions (a role with no autonomy OR no feedback cannot be motivating).

**Moderator:** the effects are stronger for individuals high in **Growth Need Strength (GNS)**.

## Up/down-rate reasoning
High tier: peer-reviewed, large multi-org sample, decades of replication. The MPS formula is
reproduced in its standard published form; the multiplicative structure (the load-bearing design
insight) is explicit in Hackman & Oldham (1976/1980).

## Reproducibility note
The five dimensions, three states, and the MPS equation are stated identically across the 1975
JDS paper, the 1976 OBHP article, and *Work Redesign* (1980); any of the three primary sources
reproduces them.
