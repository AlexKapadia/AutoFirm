- **Authors:** Eugene W. Anderson; Mary W. Sullivan
- **Year:** 1993 (Spring, Vol. 12, No. 2)
- **Venue:** *Marketing Science* (INFORMS), pp. 125–143
- **DOI:** 10.1287/mksc.12.2.125

## Ontology question informed
- **L1.B9.1** — peer-reviewed theory linking satisfaction to retention (the "why support matters" foundation).

## What the source claims (faithful)
- Develops and tests a model linking the **antecedents** and **consequences** of customer satisfaction in a utility-oriented framework, using a **nationally representative survey of 22,300 customers** of major products/services in **Sweden, 1989–1990** (the SCSB data).
- **Key finding (expectation-disconfirmation):** satisfaction is best specified as a function of **perceived quality** and **disconfirmation** — the extent to which perceived quality fails to match prepurchase expectations.
- **Notable result:** **expectations do NOT directly affect satisfaction** (contrary to much prior satisfaction literature); their effect is mediated through quality and disconfirmation.
- **Consequence:** higher satisfaction increases **repurchase intention / retention**, establishing the satisfaction→loyalty→profitability chain that underpins ACSI (source 03) and customer-success economics (source 10).

## Source-quality grade (GRADE-adapted)
- **Tier: High.** Peer-reviewed *Marketing Science* (INFORMS); large national probability sample (N=22,300); foundational, heavily-cited theory.
- **Up-rate** for: large representative sample; consistency with the later ACSI program (same research lineage / Fornell-Anderson).
- **Down-rate (minor):** single-country (Sweden) data — but the disconfirmation mechanism is broadly replicated cross-culturally, so generality holds.

## Reproducibility note
Sample size (22,300), country/period (Sweden 1989–1990), and the disconfirmation-mediation result are stated in the published abstract and paper; the satisfaction→retention link is reproducible across the broader ACSI literature.
