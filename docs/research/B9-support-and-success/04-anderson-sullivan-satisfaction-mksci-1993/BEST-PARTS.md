# BEST-PARTS — Anderson & Sullivan (1993)

## ADOPT
- **Expectation-disconfirmation as the design principle for support quality:** customer satisfaction is driven by perceived quality relative to expectations, not by absolute "delight." This justifies AutoFirm's support playbook focusing on **meeting/confirming expectations reliably** (SLA attainment, FCR) over heroic "wow" service.
  - **Build implication (L2.B9):** the playbook's quality model treats `satisfaction ≈ f(perceived_quality, disconfirmation)`; AutoFirm sets and communicates **explicit expectations** (SLA targets, ETAs) so disconfirmation stays positive. Test: scenarios where lowering promised SLA but reliably meeting it beats a high SLA frequently breached.
- **The satisfaction → retention → profitability chain** as the economic justification for investing in support/success at all — feeds the unit-economics link in source 10 (churn/NRR).

## REJECT
- **REJECT "delight"-maximisation as the default support strategy** — converges with Dixon/Toman (source 05/06): exceeding expectations yields little loyalty gain vs. reliably meeting them. Rejected on consistent High-tier evidence.

## Why (cited)
- High-tier peer-reviewed mechanism (disconfirmation) that is industry-general (CLAUDE §3.9) and directly shapes the SLA/expectation-setting design. Independently corroborates the "effortless > delightful" finding of Dixon/Toman (2010, 2013).
