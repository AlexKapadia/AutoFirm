## What the source claims (faithful)
- Built on a CEB study of **over 97,000 customers**. Reaffirms the 2010 HBR thesis: **reducing effort drives loyalty more than exceeding expectations**; the survey found **no significant difference in loyalty** between customers whose expectations were *exceeded* and those merely *met*.
- **Drivers of DISLOYALTY** (effort-increasing events): **repeat contacts, channel switching, transfers, repeating information, robotic service, and onerous policies/processes.**
- **Reported statistics (as stated in the book):**
  - **94%** of customers with **low-effort** experiences reported intent to **repurchase**, vs. **only 4%** of those with **high-effort** experiences.
  - Word of mouth is asymmetric: **~45%** of people with something positive to say tell ~3 people, while **~48%** with something negative tell ~10 people (negative WOM spreads wider).
  - **57%** of callers had first tried to solve the problem themselves on the web; **34%** are on the company website at the moment they call (channel-switching evidence).
- **Solution pillars:** reduce channel switching, prevent repeat contacts, ensure first-contact resolution (FCR), and **manage customer expectations** (set them, then meet them).

## Source-quality grade (GRADE-adapted)
- **Tier: Low–Moderate.** Practitioner book on a large proprietary dataset (N>97,000). Large sample + convergence with the peer-reviewed disconfirmation literature (source 04) up-rates the *direction*; proprietary methodology and commercial interest (CEB sells CES/effort tooling) down-rate exact figures.

## Reproducibility note

These specific percentages are CEB-internal survey results (Low-confidence as point estimates); AutoFirm relies on the *qualitative direction* (low effort → loyalty; channel-switching is common and costly) for design, not the exact numbers.
